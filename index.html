01Why Frequency Alone Isn't Enough

The classical Fourier Transform decomposes a signal into a weighted sum of eternal sinusoids. It tells you which frequencies are present, but says nothing about when they occur. For any signal whose spectral content changes over time — speech, music, vibration, radar — this is a fatal limitation.

Consider a signal containing two pure tones played in sequence: 220 Hz for the first half, then 440 Hz for the second. The Fourier Transform of the whole signal shows two sharp peaks — but you cannot tell whether the tones were sequential, simultaneous, or interleaved. All temporal structure is lost.

X(f) = ∫₋∞⁺∞ x(t) e⁻ʲ²πᶠᵗ dt
Eq. 1.1 — The Fourier Transform
X(f)Complex-valued spectrum: amplitude and phase at frequency f x(t)The time-domain signal — a real or complex function of continuous time t e⁻ʲ²πᶠᵗComplex sinusoidal basis function at frequency f (Euler's formula: cos 2πft − j sin 2πft) ∫₋∞⁺∞Integration over all time — the transform uses the entire signal, destroying temporal locality jImaginary unit (j² = −1), used in engineering convention (mathematicians write i)

The key problem is that the basis functions e⁻ʲ²πᶠᵗ extend from −∞ to +∞. Every point in time contributes equally to every frequency coefficient. A brief click and a sustained tone at the same frequency produce identical Fourier magnitudes (differing only in phase structure that is extremely difficult to interpret visually).

The Short-Time Fourier Transform solves this by a deceptively simple idea: chop the signal into short, overlapping segments, and compute the Fourier Transform of each one. By restricting our view to a local time window, each transform captures the spectral content of a brief moment.

Below it, the left plot shows the global Fourier magnitude spectrum — identical for signals with the same frequency content but different timing. The right plot shows the STFT spectrogram, which reveals the temporal evolution of spectral content.

02The STFT Equation — Symbol by Symbol

The continuous STFT is defined by multiplying the signal by a shifted window function, then taking the Fourier Transform of the product. This localises the analysis in time around the window's centre position τ.

S(τ, f) = ∫₋∞⁺∞ x(t) · w(t − τ) · e⁻ʲ²πᶠᵗ dt
Eq. 2.1 — The Continuous STFT (analysis equation)
S(τ, f)The STFT coefficient: a complex number encoding the amplitude and phase of frequency f at time τ τ (tau)Time-centre parameter — the position of the analysis window along the signal. Sliding τ produces successive spectral snapshots fFrequency variable (Hz). For each τ, S gives a full spectrum across all f x(t)Input signal. May be real-valued (audio, vibration) or complex (analytic signal, baseband comms) w(t − τ)Window function centred at time τ. Typically smooth, symmetric, and compactly supported (Hann, Gaussian, Hamming, Blackman, etc.) e⁻ʲ²πᶠᵗComplex exponential basis — extracts the component at frequency f via correlation dtInfinitesimal time element. The integral sums contributions across the window's effective support

An equivalent and illuminating form rewrites the STFT as the Fourier Transform of the windowed signal:

S(τ, f) = ℱ{ x(t) · w(t − τ) }(f)
Eq. 2.2 — STFT as Fourier Transform of a windowed excerpt

Yet another perspective: the STFT can be seen as the inner product of x(t) with a family of time–frequency atoms — translated and modulated copies of the window:

S(τ, f) = ⟨ x, w_{τ,f} ⟩ where w_{τ,f}(t) = w(t − τ) · eʲ²πᶠᵗ
Eq. 2.3 — STFT as inner product with time–frequency atoms
⟨ x, g ⟩Inner product in L²(ℝ): ∫ x(t) g*(t) dt, where * denotes complex conjugation w_{τ,f}(t)Time–frequency atom: the window w shifted to time τ and modulated to frequency f

This atom-based view connects the STFT to Gabor analysis (when w is a Gaussian) and reveals its geometric nature: each STFT coefficient measures the projection of the signal onto a localised sinusoidal "tile" in the time–frequency plane.

Why the window matters

Without the window, the integral extends over all time and we recover the ordinary Fourier Transform — a function of f alone with no τ dependence. The window's width controls the balance between time resolution and frequency resolution: a narrow window gives precise timing but blurs frequency; a wide window gives precise frequency but blurs timing. This is not a flaw of the method — it is a fundamental constraint known as the Heisenberg–Gabor uncertainty principle, explored fully in Chapter 4.

Drag the τ slider to move the analysis window along a chirp signal. The windowed excerpt (highlighted) changes, and the spectrum of that excerpt — a single column of the spectrogram — updates in real time. Notice how the peak frequency in the spectrum tracks the instantaneous frequency of the chirp.

03Window Functions — Shape, Spectrum & Trade-offs

The choice of window function profoundly affects the STFT's behaviour. Every window has a time-domain shape and a corresponding frequency-domain shape (its Fourier Transform). These two shapes are locked in a trade-off: a window that is narrow in time will be wide in frequency, and vice versa.

The rectangular window

w_rect(t) = { 1, if |t| ≤ N/2; 0, otherwise }
Eq. 3.1 — Rectangular (boxcar) window
W_rect(f) = N · sinc(fN) = N · sin(πfN) / (πfN)
Eq. 3.2 — Fourier Transform of the rectangular window
NWindow length (number of samples, or continuous duration) sinc(x)The normalised sinc function: sin(πx)/(πx). Its sidelobes cause spectral leakage

The rectangular window has the narrowest main lobe (best frequency resolution for a given length) but the worst sidelobes: the first sidelobe is only −13 dB below the main lobe, meaning nearby spectral components bleed into each other heavily.

The Hann (Hanning) window

w_hann(n) = 0.5 · (1 − cos(2πn / (N−1))), n = 0, 1, …, N−1
Eq. 3.3 — Hann window (discrete form)
nDiscrete sample index within the window, from 0 to N−1 cos(2πn/(N−1))A single period of cosine over the window's span — the raised-cosine shape 0.5The Hann window is a special case of the generalised cosine window with a₀ = 0.5, a₁ = 0.5

The Hann window's sidelobes decay at −18 dB/octave (versus −6 dB/octave for rectangular), at the cost of a main lobe that is roughly twice as wide. It is the default choice in most audio and speech processing applications.

The Gaussian window

w_gauss(t) = e^(−t² / (2σ²))
Eq. 3.4 — Gaussian window
W_gauss(f) = σ√(2π) · e^(−2π²σ²f²)
Eq. 3.5 — Fourier Transform of Gaussian window
σ (sigma)Standard deviation — controls the window width. Larger σ ⟹ wider window, narrower spectrum e^(−t²/2σ²)Gaussian bell curve. Its Fourier Transform is also Gaussian — the only function with this property

The Gaussian window is unique: it achieves the minimum possible time–frequency area product (it is the "optimal" window in the uncertainty sense). When used in the STFT, the result is called the Gabor Transform, after Dennis Gabor who introduced it in 1946.

The Hamming window

w_hamm(n) = 0.54 − 0.46 · cos(2πn / (N−1))
Eq. 3.6 — Hamming window
0.54, 0.46Coefficients chosen to minimise the first (nearest) sidelobe. The window does not reach zero at its edges, unlike Hann

The Blackman window

w_black(n) = 0.42 − 0.5 · cos(2πn/(N−1)) + 0.08 · cos(4πn/(N−1))
Eq. 3.7 — Blackman window
Three cosine termsAdding a second harmonic term further suppresses sidelobes (−58 dB first sidelobe) at the cost of a wider main lobe

04The Uncertainty Principle

The Heisenberg–Gabor uncertainty principle is the single most important theoretical result for understanding the STFT. It states that no signal analysis method — not just the STFT, but any linear time–frequency representation — can achieve arbitrarily fine resolution in both time and frequency simultaneously.

σ_t · σ_f ≥ 1 / (4π)
Eq. 4.1 — Heisenberg–Gabor uncertainty inequality
σ_tTime spread (standard deviation) of the analysis window: σ_t² = ∫ t² |w(t)|² dt / ∫ |w(t)|² dt σ_fFrequency spread (standard deviation) of the window's spectrum: σ_f² = ∫ f² |W(f)|² df / ∫ |W(f)|² df 1/(4π)The minimum possible time–frequency area. Equality is achieved only by the Gaussian window ≥Inequality — the product can never be less than this bound, no matter what window is used

Derivation sketch

Proof via the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality

1. Define the time and frequency variances about the centroid:
σ_t² = ‖t · w(t)‖² / ‖w‖² and σ_f² = ‖f · W(f)‖² / ‖W‖²
2. By Parseval's theorem, ‖W‖² = ‖w‖², so the norms can be computed in either domain.
The Fourier Transform is a unitary operator on L²(ℝ), preserving inner products and norms.
3. In the frequency domain, f · W(f) = ℱ{ (1/j2π) · w'(t) }(f)
Differentiation in time maps to multiplication by j2πf in frequency. Rearranging: f · W(f) = (1/j2π) ℱ{w'(t)}.
4. Apply Cauchy–Schwarz: |⟨ t·w, w' ⟩|² ≤ ‖t·w‖² · ‖w'‖²
The inner product ⟨t·w, w'⟩ can be evaluated by integration by parts: ⟨t·w, w'⟩ = −½‖w‖² (when w vanishes at ±∞).
5. Combining: σ_t² · σ_f² ≥ (1/4π²) · |⟨t·w, w'⟩|² / ‖w‖⁴ = 1/(16π²)
Taking square roots: σ_t · σ_f ≥ 1/(4π). ∎

What this means for the STFT

The STFT uses the same window at every frequency. This means the time–frequency tiles are all the same size. A 1024-sample window at 44.1 kHz gives about 23 ms time resolution and 43 Hz frequency resolution. These values are fixed across the entire spectrogram — low frequencies and high frequencies get the same tile size.

This is in contrast to the CQT and wavelet transforms, where the tile size varies with frequency (wide in time at low frequencies, narrow at high frequencies). The STFT's uniform tiling is both its strength (simple, invertible, well-understood) and its limitation (cannot simultaneously resolve the 23 ms attack of a snare drum and the 2 Hz vibrato of a cello).

The visualisation shows the time–frequency plane tiled by rectangles representing the STFT's resolution cells. As you increase the window length N, each tile becomes taller and narrower (better frequency resolution, worse time resolution). The area of each tile remains constant — that is the uncertainty principle in action. The dashed hyperbola shows the minimum-area bound achieved by Gaussian windows.

05Building the Spectrogram

The spectrogram is the squared magnitude of the STFT, and is by far the most common way to visualise time–frequency content. It discards phase information and represents energy density in the time–frequency plane.

P(τ, f) = |S(τ, f)|² = [Re{S(τ, f)}]² + [Im{S(τ, f)}]²
Eq. 5.1 — Spectrogram (power spectral density)
P(τ, f)Power spectrogram: energy per unit time per unit frequency at position (τ, f) |S(τ, f)|²Squared magnitude of the STFT. Also written |S|² = S · S* where S* is the complex conjugate Re{·}, Im{·}Real and imaginary parts of the complex STFT coefficient

In practice, spectrograms are usually displayed on a logarithmic (dB) scale to compress the enormous dynamic range of real-world signals:

P_dB(τ, f) = 10 · log₁₀( |S(τ, f)|² / P_ref ) [dB]
Eq. 5.2 — Spectrogram in decibels
P_refReference power level for the dB scale (often the maximum value, or a physical reference like 20 μPa for acoustics) log₁₀Common (base-10) logarithm. Each 10 dB represents a factor-of-10 change in power

The bottom panel shows the accumulating spectrogram. 06Discrete STFT & the DFT Connection

In practice, signals are sampled and the STFT is computed using the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT). The discrete STFT of a sampled signal x[n] with window w[n] of length N, hop size H, and DFT size N_FFT is:

S[m, k] = Σ_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n + mH] · w[n] · e^(−j2πkn/N_FFT)
Eq. 6.1 — Discrete STFT
mFrame index: the m-th analysis frame starts at sample mH. Each frame produces one spectral column kFrequency bin index: k = 0, 1, …, N_FFT−1. Bin k corresponds to frequency f_k = k · f_s / N_FFT HHop size (samples): the stride between successive frames. H = N means no overlap; H = N/4 (75% overlap) is common NWindow length in samples N_FFTDFT size. Often N_FFT ≥ N (zero-padding) to interpolate the spectrum for smoother visualisation x[n + mH]The signal excerpt starting at sample mH — the m-th frame w[n]The analysis window (length N, applied to each frame) f_sSample rate (Hz). The DFT resolves frequencies from 0 to f_s/2 (Nyquist)

Frequency resolution

Δf = f_s / N_FFT [Hz per bin]
Eq. 6.2 — Frequency resolution

Time resolution

Δt = H / f_s [seconds per frame]
Eq. 6.3 — Time resolution (frame spacing)

Zero-padding and spectral interpolation

When N_FFT > N, the windowed frame is padded with zeros before the DFT. This does not improve true frequency resolution (the information content is still determined by N), but it interpolates between the DFT bins, producing smoother spectral estimates. It is equivalent to evaluating the DTFT at more densely spaced frequencies.

If N_FFT = 2N: visual resolution doubles, but true resolution (main lobe width) is unchanged
Eq. 6.4 — Zero-padding: interpolation, not resolution

Relation to the DFT

Each frame of the STFT is simply a standard DFT applied to the windowed signal segment. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm computes this in O(N_FFT · log N_FFT) operations per frame. For M total frames, the entire STFT costs O(M · N_FFT · log N_FFT).

07Overlap, Hop Size & Time Resolution

The hop size H determines how densely we sample the STFT in the time direction. The overlap ratio is:

Overlap = (N − H) / N × 100%
Eq. 7.1 — Overlap ratio
NWindow length in samples HHop size in samples. H = N means 0% overlap; H = N/2 means 50%; H = N/4 means 75% OverlapFraction of window that overlaps with the adjacent frame. Higher overlap ⟹ smoother time axis, but more frames to compute

Too little overlap causes temporal aliasing: rapid changes between frames are missed. Too much overlap wastes computation on highly redundant frames. The standard recommendation for the Hann window is 50–75% overlap.

The COLA constraint

For perfect reconstruction (see Chapter 9), the windows across all frames must sum to a constant at every sample — the Constant Overlap-Add (COLA) condition:

Σ_m w[n − mH] = C, ∀ n
Eq. 7.2 — COLA condition
CA constant (independent of n). Typically normalised so that C = 1 Σ_mSum over all frame positions m. At any sample n, the overlapping windows must add to a constant

The Hann window satisfies COLA at 50% overlap (H = N/2) and 75% overlap (H = N/4). The rectangular window satisfies COLA trivially at 0% overlap (H = N). The Hamming window does not satisfy COLA at 50% overlap — an important practical consideration.

The top panel shows overlapping windows at the selected overlap ratio. The bottom panel shows their sum — when the sum is flat (constant), the COLA condition is satisfied and perfect reconstruction is possible. Watch the sum ripple as you move away from the optimal overlap for each window type.

08Magnitude, Power & Phase Spectra

The STFT coefficient S[m, k] is complex-valued. It encodes two independent quantities:

S[m, k] = |S[m, k]| · e^(jφ[m,k])
Eq. 8.1 — Polar form of the STFT
|S[m, k]|Magnitude spectrum: the amplitude of frequency bin k in frame m. Always ≥ 0 φ[m, k]Phase spectrum: the phase angle, φ = atan2(Im{S}, Re{S}), in radians (−π, π]

Magnitude vs. power

Magnitude: |S[m,k]| Power: |S[m,k]|² Log-magnitude: 20·log₁₀(|S[m,k]|) [dB]
Eq. 8.2 — Three common spectrogram scales

Phase — the neglected half

The phase spectrum is critical for signal reconstruction (Chapter 9) and encodes temporal fine structure. The instantaneous frequency can be estimated from the phase derivative:

f_inst[m, k] = f_k + (1/2πΔt) · (φ[m+1, k] − φ[m, k])_unwrapped
Eq. 8.3 — Instantaneous frequency from phase difference
f_kCentre frequency of bin k: f_k = k · f_s / N_FFT ΔtTime between successive frames: H / f_s (·)_unwrappedPhase unwrapping: adding ±2π to remove discontinuities in the phase difference f_instRefined frequency estimate within bin k — used in phase vocoders for high-quality time-stretching

Phase unwrapping is essential because the raw phase difference can alias by multiples of 2π. The phase vocoder algorithm relies on this instantaneous frequency estimate to resynthesize signals at different time scales without altering pitch.

Switch between the magnitude, phase, and instantaneous frequency views of the same signal. The magnitude spectrogram shows the familiar energy distribution. The phase spectrogram appears noisy but contains structured patterns (horizontal striations correspond to coherent tonal components). The instantaneous frequency view reveals the true frequency of each partial, refining the coarse bin-centre estimate.

09Inverse STFT & Perfect Reconstruction

A remarkable property of the STFT is that it is invertible: the original signal can be recovered exactly from its STFT coefficients, provided the analysis parameters satisfy certain conditions.

The Overlap-Add (OLA) method

x̂[n] = (1/C) · Σ_m w_s[n − mH] · IDFT{ S[m, k] }[n − mH]
Eq. 9.1 — Inverse STFT via overlap-add
x̂[n]Reconstructed signal. Equals x[n] exactly when COLA is satisfied IDFT{S[m,k]}Inverse DFT of the k-th frame: converts spectral coefficients back to a time-domain frame w_s[n]Synthesis window. May equal the analysis window, or may differ (e.g., dual window for WOLA) CThe COLA sum from Eq. 7.2. Dividing by C normalises the reconstruction Σ_mSum over all frames — each contributes a windowed time-domain segment, and they overlap-add to reconstruct x

The Weighted Overlap-Add (WOLA) method

When the synthesis window differs from the analysis window, perfect reconstruction requires:

Σ_m w_a[n − mH] · w_s[n − mH] = 1, ∀ n
Eq. 9.2 — WOLA perfect reconstruction condition
w_aAnalysis window (used in the forward STFT) w_sSynthesis window (used in the inverse STFT). The product w_a · w_s must satisfy COLA

A common choice: use a square-root Hann window for both analysis and synthesis. Since √hann × √hann = hann, and Hann satisfies COLA at 50% overlap, this gives perfect reconstruction with symmetric treatment of analysis and synthesis.

Parseval's relation for the STFT

Σ_n |x[n]|² = (1/C) · Σ_m Σ_k |S[m, k]|²
Eq. 9.3 — Energy conservation (Parseval/Plancherel for STFT)

This guarantees that the total signal energy is preserved in the time–frequency representation — no energy is created or lost by the analysis–synthesis cycle.

10Real-World Applications

The STFT is the backbone of modern signal processing. Its simplicity, invertibility, and well-understood mathematical properties make it the default choice for an extraordinary range of applications:

Speech and audio processing

Virtually all modern speech recognition systems, text-to-speech engines, and audio codecs use the STFT as their front-end. The mel-frequency spectrogram — an STFT followed by a mel-scale filterbank — is the standard input to speech AI models. Noise reduction algorithms (spectral subtraction, Wiener filtering) operate on the STFT magnitude while preserving the noisy phase.

The phase vocoder

Time-stretching and pitch-shifting of audio are accomplished by manipulating STFT frames. To slow down a signal by factor α, one simply spaces the synthesis frames further apart (increasing the synthesis hop to αH) while adjusting phase to maintain coherence. This is the foundation of every "slow-down-without-pitch-change" feature in audio software.

Spectral analysis in engineering

Vibration monitoring of rotating machinery, modal analysis of structures, EEG/EMG biomedical signal analysis, sonar and radar processing, and telecommunications channel estimation all rely on the STFT or its close variant Welch's method (averaged periodograms from overlapping STFT frames).

P_Welch(f) = (1/M) · Σ_{m=0}^{M-1} |S[m, f]|²
Eq. 10.1 — Welch's method: averaged STFT power spectra
P_Welch(f)Estimated power spectral density at frequency f — lower variance than a single periodogram MNumber of overlapping frames averaged. More frames ⟹ smoother estimate, but requires longer signals

A continuously running STFT spectrogram displays three selectable signals. Pitched content (melodies, sustained tones) appears as horizontal bands — their frequency is stable over time. Transient content (drum hits, clicks) appears as vertical splashes — energy spread across all frequencies at a single moment. The mixed signal shows both patterns simultaneously, demonstrating why the STFT is universally used as the first stage of audio analysis.
